Curtis Dean🥇

My responsibilities for this project were largely related to EDA, plus any associated visualizations, and git workflow. I utilized R’s Tidyverse (mostly dplyr and ggplot) packages in my analysis. The first step was to determine how much of the data to use. I analyzed the percentage of missing values in biometric categories (age, height, weight) vs. the percentage of the overall data and developed a staggered bar chart to make the decision-making easier. I recommended 1964 as the cutoff point, since it allowed us to remove around 85% of the missing values, while maintaining 70% of the overall data points. Next, we needed to determine how to handle Olympic sports/events. For example, Alpine Skiing (sport) has 6 different events. In some cases, it was reasonable to collapse the different events into a single “event category,” while other times the sport was too broad and not at all representative of the different events. These sports were identified by looking at the summary statistics of the biometric data for each event, partitioned by “sex” of the participants. We ultimately decided to remove “team sports” (like Baseball) from the dataset and create subcategories for sports with large variances in biometric data across the associated events. Finally, I created our GitHub repository and have been responsible for creating/merging feature branches and dictating git workflow.

Mona Elahi🥇

Hi, I’m Mona. For this project I have worked on a machine learning prediction model of winning a medal based on a person’s biometric data. I first label encoded the data, then split the dataframe into winter and summer data and then trained and tested the data. I created a make_pipeline and used Iterative Imputer, StandardScaler and models in the pipeline. The two models I used were Random Forest Classifier and Decision Tree Classifier for predicting a person’s chances of winning a medal, which gave a very low recall score. i.e., for winter RFC 31% and DTC 35% and for summer RFC 26% and DTC 34%. To improve the performance of our model, I used the combination of oversampling the minority (Medal) class and undersampling the majority (no medal) class. I applied SMOTE for over sampling and RandomUnderSampler for under sampling, which gave me 69% for winter and 65% for summer data. I absolutely enjoyed working with the immensely talented team.
